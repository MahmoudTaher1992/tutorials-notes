# Product Manager Complete Study Guide (Ideal / Angel Method)
## Part 1: Ideal Product Manager — Foundation, Discovery & User Research

---

### 1. The Role & Mindset

#### 1.1 Defining the Product Manager
- 1.1.1 The PM mandate — maximize product value, not feature output
- 1.1.2 "CEO of the product" — myth vs reality (no authority, all accountability)
- 1.1.3 The PM triangle — business viability, user desirability, technical feasibility
- 1.1.4 PM vs Project Manager — outcome ownership vs timeline coordination
- 1.1.5 PM vs Product Owner (PO) — strategy + discovery vs backlog execution
- 1.1.6 PM vs Product Designer — what/why vs how it looks and feels
- 1.1.7 Influence without authority — the core PM superpower

#### 1.2 PM Career Ladder
- 1.2.1 APM (Associate PM) — structured programs, task-driven, mentored
- 1.2.2 PM — owns a surface/feature, full discovery-to-delivery cycle
- 1.2.3 Senior PM — owns a product or large domain, more ambiguity
- 1.2.4 Group PM / Principal PM — manages a product area, mentors PMs
- 1.2.5 Director of Product — manages PM team, cross-product strategy
- 1.2.6 VP of Product / CPO — full product portfolio, company strategy

#### 1.3 PM Specializations
- 1.3.1 Growth PM — acquisition, activation, retention, monetization funnels
- 1.3.2 Platform PM — internal APIs, developer tools, infrastructure products
- 1.3.3 Technical PM — deep eng collaboration, API products, data pipelines
- 1.3.4 B2B/Enterprise PM — sales-assisted, complex buyers, contract cycles
- 1.3.5 B2C / Consumer PM — self-serve, mass market, psychology-driven
- 1.3.6 Data PM — AI/ML products, model evaluation, data quality

#### 1.4 Core PM Skills
- 1.4.1 Strategic thinking — systems view, long-term vs short-term tradeoffs
- 1.4.2 Customer empathy — genuine curiosity about user problems
- 1.4.3 Data literacy — SQL basics, interpreting charts, avoiding p-hacking
- 1.4.4 Technical literacy — understanding APIs, architecture tradeoffs, feasibility
- 1.4.5 Communication — written clarity, verbal precision, storytelling with data
- 1.4.6 Prioritization — saying no with reasoning, managing competing demands
- 1.4.7 Decisiveness — shipping imperfect over waiting for perfect

### 2. Problem Discovery & Opportunity Identification

#### 2.1 Sources of Discovery
- 2.1.1 Customer interviews — the primary source of unfiltered truth
- 2.1.2 Support tickets & NPS verbatims — pain points at scale
- 2.1.3 Sales call recordings — objections, competitive comparisons
- 2.1.4 Product analytics — where users drop off, what they actually do
- 2.1.5 Competitive analysis — feature gaps, positioning opportunities
- 2.1.6 Market trends — regulatory shifts, technology disruption, macro forces
- 2.1.7 Internal stakeholders — sales, CS, engineering signal aggregation

#### 2.2 Problem Framing
- 2.2.1 Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) — functional, emotional, social jobs
- 2.2.2 "The Mom Test" (Rob Fitzpatrick) — ask about life, not your product
- 2.2.3 Problem statement format — user + pain + context + current behavior
- 2.2.4 Opportunity canvas — problem, user, solution space, business case
- 2.2.5 Five Whys — root cause behind surface complaints
- 2.2.6 How Might We (HMW) — converting problems to opportunity questions
- 2.2.7 Separating problem from solution — avoid jumping to solutions prematurely

#### 2.3 Continuous Discovery (Teresa Torres)
- 2.3.1 Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) — goal → opportunities → solutions → experiments
- 2.3.2 Weekly interview cadence — minimum 1 customer interview per week
- 2.3.3 Assumption mapping — identify and test riskiest assumptions first
- 2.3.4 Experiment design — small, fast tests before building
- 2.3.5 Shared discovery — whole team (PM + designer + engineer) attends research

### 3. User Research

#### 3.1 Research Methods Overview
- 3.1.1 Generative research — understand the problem space (before ideating)
- 3.1.2 Evaluative research — test solutions against real users
- 3.1.3 Qualitative methods — interviews, ethnography, diary studies
- 3.1.4 Quantitative methods — surveys, analytics, A/B tests
- 3.1.5 Attitudinal vs behavioral — what users say vs what they do
- 3.1.6 When to use each method — maturity of the problem/solution matters

#### 3.2 User Interviews
- 3.2.1 Recruiting — screener criteria, incentives, 5-8 participants for saturation
- 3.2.2 Interview guide — open-ended questions, story-based prompts
- 3.2.3 Active listening — silence, follow-up probes, avoiding leading questions
- 3.2.4 Avoid validation traps — don't ask "would you use this?"
- 3.2.5 Note-taking systems — tagging insights, video timestamps
- 3.2.6 Synthesis — affinity mapping, insight clustering, pattern identification

#### 3.3 Surveys & Quantitative Research
- 3.3.1 Survey design — Likert scales, NPS, semantic differential
- 3.3.2 Sampling bias — self-selection, non-response, survivorship
- 3.3.3 Statistical significance — sample size, p-value basics, practical significance
- 3.3.4 NPS (Net Promoter Score) — promoters - detractors, benchmarking, action planning
- 3.3.5 CSAT & CES — customer satisfaction and effort scores
- 3.3.6 Intercept surveys — in-product surveys at key moments

#### 3.4 Synthesis & Sense-Making
- 3.4.1 Affinity mapping — group raw insights into themes (KJ method)
- 3.4.2 User personas — data-driven, behavior-based, not demographic-based
- 3.4.3 Proto-personas — hypothesis-based for early-stage, to be validated
- 3.4.4 Empathy maps — say, think, feel, do — for cross-team alignment
- 3.4.5 Customer journey maps — stages, touchpoints, emotions, pain points
- 3.4.6 Experience maps — broader than journey, includes off-product touchpoints
- 3.4.7 Service blueprints — front-stage (user) + back-stage (business) view

#### 3.5 Competitive & Market Research
- 3.5.1 Market sizing — TAM, SAM, SOM — bottom-up vs top-down
- 3.5.2 Competitive landscape — direct, indirect, substitute, future entrants
- 3.5.3 Feature comparison matrix — capabilities grid, gap analysis
- 3.5.4 SWOT analysis — strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, threats
- 3.5.5 Porter's Five Forces — supplier power, buyer power, rivalry, substitutes, new entrants
- 3.5.6 Blue Ocean Strategy — create uncontested market space, eliminate/reduce/raise/create
- 3.5.7 PESTLE — Political, Economic, Social, Tech, Legal, Environmental macro analysis
- 3.5.8 Gartner Hype Cycle — technology maturity positioning
