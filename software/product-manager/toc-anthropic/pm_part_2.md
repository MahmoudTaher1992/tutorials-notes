# Product Manager Complete Study Guide (Ideal / Angel Method)
## Part 2: Ideal Product Manager — Strategy, Roadmapping & Prioritization

---

### 4. Product Strategy

#### 4.1 Vision & Mission
- 4.1.1 Product vision — 3-5 year aspirational north star, emotionally resonant
- 4.1.2 Product mission — the purpose and method for achieving the vision
- 4.1.3 Vision vs strategy vs roadmap — hierarchy of abstraction
- 4.1.4 Writing a compelling vision — narrative over bullet points, "working backwards"
- 4.1.5 Aligning vision to company strategy — OKR cascade, mission alignment
- 4.1.6 Communicating vision — town halls, product briefs, all-hands, team rituals

#### 4.2 Strategy Frameworks
- 4.2.1 Value Proposition Canvas — customer profile (jobs/pains/gains) + value map
- 4.2.2 Business Model Canvas — 9-block Osterwalder model
- 4.2.3 Good Strategy / Bad Strategy (Rumelt) — diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions
- 4.2.4 Crossing the Chasm (Moore) — early adopter → early majority gap
- 4.2.5 Technology Adoption Lifecycle — innovators, early adopters, early/late majority, laggards
- 4.2.6 Disruption theory (Christensen) — low-end vs new-market disruption
- 4.2.7 Moats & defensibility — network effects, switching costs, economies of scale, IP

#### 4.3 OKRs & Goal Setting
- 4.3.1 OKR structure — 1 Objective + 2-5 Key Results (measurable outcomes, not tasks)
- 4.3.2 Product OKRs vs company OKRs — cascading vs bottoms-up
- 4.3.3 North Star Metric — single metric that captures core value delivery
- 4.3.4 Input metrics vs output metrics — leading vs lagging indicators
- 4.3.5 OKR anti-patterns — too many objectives, KRs as task lists, sandbagging
- 4.3.6 OKR cadence — annual north star, quarterly OKRs, monthly review, weekly KPI check

#### 4.4 Go-to-Market Strategy
- 4.4.1 GTM components — positioning, messaging, channels, pricing, sales motion
- 4.4.2 Positioning — differentiated framing in the customer's mind (Ries & Trout)
- 4.4.3 Messaging hierarchy — headline → value props → proof points
- 4.4.4 ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) — firmographic + behavioral + psychographic
- 4.4.5 Sales motion — self-serve, inside sales, enterprise field sales, partner-led
- 4.4.6 Channel strategy — owned, earned, paid, partner
- 4.4.7 Pricing models — freemium, subscription, usage-based, seat-based, enterprise

### 5. Roadmapping

#### 5.1 Roadmap Types
- 5.1.1 Outcome-based roadmap — goals and metrics, not features (Now/Next/Later)
- 5.1.2 Feature-based roadmap — specific deliverables with dates, high stakeholder pressure
- 5.1.3 Theme-based roadmap — strategic bets grouped by theme
- 5.1.4 Opportunity roadmap — problem space mapped, not solutions
- 5.1.5 Public vs internal roadmap — external for trust, internal for alignment
- 5.1.6 Quarterly roadmap vs annual plan — confidence decay over time horizon

#### 5.2 Roadmap Process
- 5.2.1 Input gathering — strategy, user research, analytics, stakeholder requests
- 5.2.2 Prioritization integration — frameworks applied before roadmap construction
- 5.2.3 Roadmap reviews — quarterly stakeholder sessions, monthly team check-ins
- 5.2.4 Communicating changes — explaining re-prioritization, not apologizing for it
- 5.2.5 Continuous roadmapping — treat as living document, not annual artifact
- 5.2.6 Roadmap tooling — Productboard, Aha!, Linear, Notion, simple spreadsheets

#### 5.3 Requirements Writing

##### 5.3.1 Product Requirements Documents (PRDs)
- Problem statement — what problem, for whom, why now
- Success metrics — how we'll know it worked
- Scope — in/out of scope, explicit non-goals
- User stories — functional requirements from user perspective
- Edge cases & error states — explicitly documented
- Open questions — unresolved decisions, dependencies

##### 5.3.2 User Stories
- Format — "As a [persona], I want [action] so that [value]"
- INVEST criteria — Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable
- Acceptance criteria — Given/When/Then format, testable
- Job Stories — "When [situation], I want [motivation], so I can [outcome]"
- Story mapping — horizontal flow of user journey + vertical slices of detail
- Definition of Done — shared understanding of completion

### 6. Prioritization

#### 6.1 Prioritization Principles
- 6.1.1 Saying no is a product skill — not all requests deserve a yes
- 6.1.2 Opportunity cost — choosing X means not choosing Y
- 6.1.3 Business value vs user value — both must be weighed
- 6.1.4 Reversibility — prefer reversible decisions first
- 6.1.5 Effort asymmetry — small effort + large impact = always prioritize first
- 6.1.6 Urgency vs importance — Eisenhower matrix applied to product

#### 6.2 Prioritization Frameworks
- 6.2.1 RICE — Reach × Impact × Confidence / Effort — numerical scoring
- 6.2.2 ICE — Impact × Confidence × Ease — simpler variant of RICE
- 6.2.3 MoSCoW — Must-have, Should-have, Could-have, Won't-have — for scope negotiation
- 6.2.4 Kano Model — Basic (expected), Performance (linear), Delight (unexpected) needs
- 6.2.5 Cost of Delay — value of shipping NOW vs shipping later, WSJF
- 6.2.6 Value vs Effort matrix — 2×2 quick prioritization for low-data environments
- 6.2.7 Opportunity Scoring — importance vs satisfaction gap (Ulwick)
- 6.2.8 Weighted scoring — custom weights per dimension (revenue, retention, delight)

#### 6.3 Backlog Management
- 6.3.1 Grooming/refinement — weekly, size: enough for 2 upcoming sprints
- 6.3.2 Backlog hygiene — archive stale items, re-estimate quarterly
- 6.3.3 Bug vs feature trade-offs — defect-to-feature ratio policy
- 6.3.4 Technical debt in backlog — PM ownership of tech debt prioritization
- 6.3.5 User story mapping — horizontal workflow + vertical detail slices
- 6.3.6 Icebox — parking lot for ideas, not dead ideas, revisited quarterly

#### 6.4 Handling Competing Priorities
- 6.4.1 Stakeholder pressure management — data-backed trade-off conversations
- 6.4.2 HiPPO effect — Highest Paid Person's Opinion vs data-driven decisions
- 6.4.3 The disagree-and-commit pattern — move forward after making decision explicit
- 6.4.4 Escalation protocol — when to escalate vs absorb the decision
- 6.4.5 Rapid re-prioritization — incident, market shift, exec request handling
